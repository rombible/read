Offerings

*Voluntary Sacrifices

Burnt offering (olah, literally, "an offering of ascent")

Purposes: A voluntary act of worship to express devotion or commitment and complete surrender to God, also used as an atonement for unintentional sin.

Elements: Bull, ram or bird (dove or young pigeon for the poor); wholly consumed; no defect

God: the meat and bones and organs of the animal were to be totally burnt.

Levites: The animal's skin to sell it later

 

Grain offering (minchah)

Purposes : to express thanksgiving in recognition of God's provision and unmerited goodwill toward the person

  accompanied burnt offering and peace offering (along with drink offering)

Elements : the fruit of the field in the form of a cake or baked bread made of grain, fine flour, and oil and salt (cooked bread-baked (2:4), grilled (2:5), fried (2:7), roasted, or made into cereal (2:14)-

  though always seasoned (2:13), unsweetened, and unleavened (2:11)). no yeast or honey

  The grain offering was one of the sacrifices accompanied by a drink offering of one-quarter hin (about a quart) of wine, which was poured into the fire on the altar (Numbers 15:4-5).

  only a portion of the offering was to be burnt (2:9).

God: Entire portion(Priest's own offering), Others (Memorial portion - a handful)

Priests :  Others(all the remainder, but it had to be eaten within the court of the tabernacle.)

 

Peace offering (shelem)

Purpose: thanksgiving and fellowship followed by a shared meal.

 The vow offering, thanksgiving offering, and freewill offering mentioned in the Old Testament (Thanksgiving Offerings (Lev 7:12), Freewill Offerings (7:16), and Wave Offerings (7:30)) were all peace offerings.

 to consecrate a meal between two or more parties before God and share that meal together in fellowship of peace and a commitment to each others' future prosperity.

 Voluntary act of worship; fellowship (it included a communal meal);

Elements: any unblemished animal from the worshiper's herd, and/or various grains or breads.

 These pieces of the offering were called the "wave offering" and the "heave offering" because they were waved or lifted over the altar during the ceremony.

God : The fat, kidneys, and lobe of the liver (burnt),

The high priest : the breast of the animal (wave offering)

The officiating priest; the right foreleg (heave offering)

The participants : Thanksgiving offering (all the remainder to be eaten the same day; no left over allowed)

 Vow or freewill offering (remainder to be eaten the same day and the next day); any left over to be burnt on the 3rd day)

 

*Mandatory Sacrifices

Sin offering (chattath, literally "sin" or "sin offering")

Purpose : to atone for sin and cleanse from defilement. as an offering of atonement for unintentional sin (4:2-3, 4:20).

 viewed as guilt offering, removing the consequences for lack of perfection (4:13-14, 4:22-23).

 As an atonement offering, it contained elements of a Burnt Offering (4:25), yet at the same time had elements of a Peace Offering (4:26).

 Conversely, some of the "sins" for which one needed atonement were not moral sins but rather matters of ritual impurity (5:1-5).

 As such, some have proposed the term "Purification Offering" instead of "Sin Offering."

 "Purification Offering" - the primary purpose of this offering is not to atone for sins but rather to purify oneself for re-entering the presence of God. 

Elements : five possible elements of a sin sacrifice-a young bull, a male goat, a female goat, a dove/pigeon, or 1/10 ephah of fine flour.

 The type of animal depended on the identity and financial situation of the giver.

 A female goat was the sin offering for the common person, fine flour was the sacrifice of the very poor, a young bull was offered for the high priest and the congregation as a whole, and so on.

 The elements of a Purification Offering could be any of the elements of the previous three types offerings, though unlike the Peace Offering, the meal was not to be shared by the one offering the sacrifice.

  Young bull: for High Priest and whole congregation. The blood was to be sprinkled in front of the veil and put on the horns of the altar of incense (see notes)

  Male goat: for leader. The blood was to be put on the horns of the altar of burnt offering

  Female goat or lamb: for common person. The blood was to be put on the horns of the altar of burnt offering

  Dove or pigeon: for the poor. The blood was to be put on the horns of the altar of burnt offering

God : Fatty portions (fat covering inner parts; fat tail, kidneys, lobe of the liver)

  the rest of the animal was either totally burned on the altar and the ashes thrown outside the camp (in atoning for the high priest and congregation), or eaten within the tabernacle court.

  1. Atonement for High Priest and congregation: none (all the remainder was to be burnt outside the camp where the ashes were thrown)

  2. Atonement for others: all the remainder (had to be eaten within court of tabernacle)

 

Trespass offering (asham)

Purpose : atonement for unintentional sins that required reimbursement to an offended party, and also as a cleansing from defiling sins or physical maladies.

  Does not refer to a matter of one's conscience but rather to something one owes on account of a "sin."

  Make restitution; pay 20% fine

Element : exclusively a ram.

  his offering had a specific monetary value, and one who owed another on account of a debt due to a "sin" could repay it in silver rather than by sacrificing a ram (5:15).

  In addition, a 20% fee was assessed and given to the priest who mitigated the debt (5:16).

God: Fatty portions (fat covering inner parts; fat tail, kidneys, lobe of the liver)

Priests: the remainder of the ram had to be eaten inside the court of the tabernacle.

 

"When more than one kind of offering was presented (as in Numbers 6:16-17), the procedure was usually as follows:

1.             sin offering or trespass offering,

2.             burnt offering,

3.             peace offering and grain offering (along with a drink offering).

 

Daily the priest offered 1 lamb in the morning service and 1 lamb in the evening service. On a Sabbath, 2 additional lambs were offered after the morning service.

High days: seven high days are designated in Leviticus 23 (verses 7, 8, 21, 25, 28, 30-32, 35-36). They were to be treated like the seventh day Sabbath, even though they could occur on a day other than the seventh day (Saturday) of the week.

On a high day, besides the daily regular offerings, the priest offered 2 additional lambs after the morning service, and additional sacrifices for the day. These seven high days were:

1.             The 1st day of the Feast of Unleavened Bread (Nisan 15)

2.             The 7th day of the Feast of Unleavened Bread (Nisan 21)

3.             The Feast of Weeks (Sivan 6)

4.             The Feast of Trumpets (Tishri 1)

5.             The Day of Atonement (Tishri 10)

6.             The 1st day of the Feast of Tabernacles (Tishri 15)

7.             The Last Great Day (Tishri 22)
